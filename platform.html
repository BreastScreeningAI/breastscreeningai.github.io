Platform

AI assistance designed around the radiologist

BreastScreening-AI is a human-centred research platform exploring how artificial intelligence can support breast imaging review, second reading, triage and clinical documentation while clinicians retain control.

What it supports

A focused layer of assistance within clinical review

The platform brings imaging, AI-generated observations and clinician interaction into a common research environment. Capabilities are evaluated progressively and may differ by study, site and software configuration.

Image review

Presents breast imaging and supporting information in an interface designed around radiology tasks.

Second reading

Explores AI assistance as an additional perspective after or alongside the clinician's own assessment.

Triage research

Investigates whether decision support can help prioritise attention without replacing formal clinical classification.

Explanations

Studies how contextual explanations and visible AI outputs affect confidence, interpretation and decision-making.

Reporting support

Examines structured reporting and documentation patterns that preserve uncertainty and accountability.

Interaction evidence

Captures research measures related to usability, workflow, decision stability and human-AI collaboration.

Imaging context

Multimodal by research direction

BreastScreening-AI's research lineage covers mammography, ultrasound and magnetic resonance imaging. Current evidence is not uniform across modalities: the 2026 Hospital da Luz exploratory pilot evaluated a mammography component, while broader multimodal integration remains an active development and validation direction.

Evidence boundary

References to multimodality describe the platform's research scope and intended direction. They do not mean that every modality has completed equivalent clinical validation or received regulatory authorisation.

Design principles

Clinical judgement remains central

The platform is developed as assistive research software. Its design priorities reflect the need for traceability, appropriate reliance and accountable human decision-making.

Human control

The clinician reviews the case, interprets the available information and remains responsible for the clinical decision.

Visible uncertainty

Research explores ways to communicate AI outputs without presenting uncertain predictions as established facts.

Workflow fit

Interfaces and integrations are evaluated in relation to existing radiology tasks, systems and time constraints.

Evidence separation

Current pilot findings, previous publications and future objectives are reported as distinct evidence categories.

Privacy and security

Clinical research requires controlled access, appropriate de-identification and institution-specific governance.

Iterative validation

Usability, technical integration and clinical performance require separate, progressively broader evaluation.

Current status

A validated prototype progressing toward broader demonstration

What is supported today

  • Integration and usability work in representative clinical environments.
  • An exploratory Hospital da Luz pilot involving seven physicians.
  • Research activities covering human-AI interaction and structured clinical reporting.
  • Continued technical, clinical and governance preparation for broader studies.
