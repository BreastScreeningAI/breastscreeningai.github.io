Platform

Breast imaging AI, measured through human-AI collaboration

BreastScreening-AI is a clinician-centred research platform for studying multimodal image review, AI-assisted classification and personalized explanations. Published studies evaluate how the technology changes decisions, time, workload, trust and usability while the clinician retains final authority.

Platform model

One research environment, four connected layers

The platform combines clinical images, computational models, explainable interaction and clinician feedback. Each layer has a different evidence base, and availability varies by study, modality and site.

Multimodal review

Research interfaces bring mammography, ultrasound, MRI and relevant case information into coordinated review workflows.

AI second reader

Classification and lesion-localization models provide an additional perspective that clinicians may accept, reject or disregard.

Personalized explanations

Communication style, supporting arguments and model outputs can be adapted to different levels of clinical experience.

Measurable interaction

Studies capture decision accuracy, false positives, false negatives, reading time, workload, trust and usability.

Imaging research

Performance varies by task, modality and dataset

The publications show why a single headline accuracy is not an adequate description of the platform. Results must remain connected to their dataset, endpoint and validation design.

Design implications

The numbers shape how the platform should behave

The evidence favors assistance that is visible, adjustable and accountable rather than automation that obscures uncertainty or removes clinician agency.

Retain clinician control

Clinicians must be able to inspect, accept, reject or disregard an AI recommendation and remain responsible for the final assessment.

Adapt communication

In a 52-clinician study, novices achieved the highest correct-decision rate with assertive guidance, while experts performed best with a suggestive tone.

Expose uncertainty

Performance changes substantially across BI-RADS categories, modalities and datasets; the interface should not present confidence as certainty.

Measure workflow

Reading time, workload and usability matter alongside model performance, but faster interaction must not be assumed to mean safer diagnosis.

Protect traceability

Research records should distinguish the original clinician assessment, AI output, final decision and relevant case context.

Validate progressively

Technical performance, human factors, prospective clinical utility and health-economic value require separate stages of evidence.

Evidence boundaries

Promising research evidence, not clinical authorization

The current portfolio is strongest in human-AI interaction and prototype workflow evaluation. Broader clinical and economic claims require prospective, independent and multicenter validation.

Supported by the portfolio

  • Published clinician studies involving cohorts of 31, 45 and 52 participants.
  • Controlled improvements in selected task-time and classification measures.
  • Multimodal imaging and personalized-explanation research.
  • Project-level TRL 5 progressing toward TRL 6.

Not yet demonstrated

  • Prospective improvement in cancer detection or interval-cancer rates.
  • Fewer recalls, biopsies or delayed treatments in routine care.
  • Improved survival, quality of life or patient-reported outcomes.
  • Regulatory authorization, reimbursement, cost savings or return on investment.

Current development position

BreastScreening-AI remains research software under progressive technical, human-factors and clinical evaluation. The approved maturity statement is project-level TRL 5 progressing toward TRL 6.
