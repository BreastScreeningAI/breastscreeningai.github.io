Clinical workflow

Decision support with the clinician in control

The intended workflow introduces AI assistance into breast imaging review without transferring clinical responsibility to the software. Exact steps depend on the study protocol, institution and technical integration.

Intended pathway

From case access to accountable documentation

This conceptual pathway describes the role being designed and evaluated. It is not a claim that every step is deployed at every partner institution.

Step 1

Access the case

The clinician opens the available breast imaging and relevant case information within the approved clinical or research environment.

Step 2

Review independently

The clinician performs an initial assessment using professional judgement and the institution's established process.

Step 3

Inspect AI assistance

AI outputs may provide an additional observation, explanation or triage perspective for consideration.

Step 4

Reconcile findings

The clinician compares the available information, resolves disagreement and may accept, reject or disregard the AI output.

Step 5

Document the decision

The clinician records the assessment through the applicable reporting process and remains accountable for the result.

Human oversight

Clear boundaries for appropriate use

The distinction between assistance and authority is essential to the platform's intended research positioning.

The platform may support

  • Reviewing and organising available case information.
  • Providing an additional AI-assisted perspective.
  • Exploring second-reading and triage workflows.
  • Supporting structured documentation research.
  • Recording interaction data for approved studies.

The platform does not replace

  • A qualified clinician's interpretation.
  • Institutional protocols or multidisciplinary review.
  • Formal BI-RADS assessment and clinical judgement.
  • Regulatory, safety or quality-management obligations.
  • Emergency services, diagnosis or medical advice.

Integration context

Designed to meet the workflow where it operates

Clinical AI depends on more than a model. Useful evaluation requires imaging access, identity and permission controls, interface fit, secure infrastructure, traceable outputs and institution-specific governance.

Clinical systems

Research integrations may involve PACS-adjacent environments or approved application platforms, depending on the site.

Case preparation

Study datasets and identifiers require protocol-led selection, de-identification and controlled handling.

Access controls

Permissions, network configuration and user access must be agreed with the participating institution.

Interface validation

Display, navigation and interaction changes are tested because they can affect study validity and clinical use.

Traceability

Research activities should distinguish the clinician's input, AI output, final decision and relevant context.

Local governance

Ethics, privacy, cybersecurity and clinical responsibilities remain specific to each deployment or study.

Evidence context

What the current pilot contributes

The Hospital da Luz exploratory pilot assessed a mammography workflow with seven physicians. Its clearest reported effect was an improvement in a complementary triage-level analysis. Other clinical endpoints did not show statistically significant change, and the small study should not be interpreted as proof of clinical efficacy.

Most paired observations remained unchanged with AI assistance. Both improved and worsened decisions were observed, reinforcing the need for human oversight and broader validation.
