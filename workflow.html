Clinical workflow impact

Turn published study effects into a local pilot calculator

The figures below translate reported research outcomes into operational hypotheses. They are useful for validation planning, not claims of guaranteed routine-care performance.

69 sec

Mean time difference

Controlled research reported mean task time decreasing from 377 to 308 seconds, an 18.3% reduction. Per 10,000 reads, that equals about 191.7 clinician hours.

42.1 sec

Personalized interaction gain

Assertiveness-based communication reduced mean task time from 166.12 to 124.02 seconds, or 25.3%, with p = 0.005 and r = 0.49.

27 pp

False-positive reduction

False positives decreased from 54% to 27% in a controlled clinician-AI condition, a 50% relative reduction that should be validated against local recall and workup policies.

4 pp

False-negative reduction

False negatives decreased from 6% to 2% in the same evidence stream, a 66.7% relative reduction. Prospective safety validation is still required.

91%

Positive clinician response

Clinicians reported positive expectations and perceptive satisfaction, supporting adoption planning when training, explanations and governance are designed well.

96.67%

MRI weak-supervision accuracy

The weakly supervised MRI model reported 96.67% accuracy, AUC 0.96, sensitivity 0.94, specificity 1.00 and precision 1.00, suggesting potential for attention support in complex MRI review.

Pathway map

From image acquisition to accountable final decision

This page is intentionally different from the platform page. The platform explains the product. The workflow page explains where the product sits, what it measures and where the clinician stays in control.

01 Intake

Case readiness

Indication, prior exams, risk factors, modality availability and local protocol are checked before the reader opens the case.

02 MG

Screening base

Mammography anchors population screening and first-line review, including density-aware interpretation and comparison with priors.

03 US

Targeted workup

Ultrasound clarifies palpable or imaging-detected findings and supports lesion characterization or biopsy guidance.

04 MRI

Complex review

MRI is used when high sensitivity, disease extent, high-risk screening or treatment-planning detail is clinically needed.

05 AI

Second-reader signal

The system can provide classification, localization, explanation and disagreement cues inside a controlled validation pathway.

06 Report

Final accountability

The clinician records the final interpretation while the pilot logs timing, decision changes, trust, workload and safety events.

Modality logic

Why radiology needs MG, US and MRI in the same workflow story

Multimodality is not a decorative product feature. It reflects how breast imaging decisions are actually assembled from screening, targeted characterization and selected high-sensitivity escalation.

MG

Mammography

Best positioned as the scalable screening foundation. Workflow value comes from consistent density-aware review, suspicious-region attention and comparison with priors.

Workflow pressure: high volume.
US

Ultrasound

Best positioned as the targeted clarification layer. Workflow value comes from organizing follow-up reasoning, characterizing findings and supporting interventional planning.

Workflow pressure: case-by-case variability.
MRI

MRI

Best positioned as the complex-case escalation layer. Workflow value comes from reducing cognitive burden across volumes and sequences while preserving expert judgement.

Workflow pressure: information overload.

Clinical context: mammography, breast ultrasound, breast MRI and the project publications listed on the Publications page. Modality choice remains patient-, indication- and protocol-specific.

Decision loop

Make AI useful without making it the decision-maker

The safest workflow separates first read, AI assistance, reconciliation and final documentation. That structure also makes the clinical impact measurable.

1

Independent read

Clinician reviews the case and records an initial assessment when the validation protocol requires a baseline.

2

AI evidence

The system presents classification, heatmap, confidence, modality-specific cue or personalized explanation.

3

Reconciliation

Clinician accepts, rejects or investigates the AI signal, especially when there is disagreement or uncertainty.

4

Final report

The clinician remains accountable; the system logs time, change, modality and decision status for audit.

Trust calibration

Published adoption work points to security, risk and trust as major determinants of intelligent-agent use in medical imaging.

Explanation design

Heatmaps, contextual clinical arguments and personalized communication should be tuned to experience level and clinical task.

Overreliance control

Governance should monitor whether readers ignore, over-trust or appropriately negotiate AI recommendations.

Deployment checklist

What must be true before claiming workflow impact

The clinical workflow impact should be earned through staged evidence, not assumed from a lab result.

Evidence setup

  • Lock the intended-use statement.
  • Define eligible modalities and case types.
  • Record clinician-only and clinician-AI phases.
  • Predefine success, safety and stopping rules.

Measurement setup

  • Capture reading time and report turnaround.
  • Track decision changes and disagreement.
  • Measure trust, workload, usability and training burden.
  • Link workflow outcomes to local cost assumptions.

Governance setup

  • Keep clinician override visible and easy.
  • Audit AI outputs, uncertainty and failures.
  • Review performance by modality, site and reader seniority.
  • Document regulatory, privacy and cybersecurity controls.

Human oversight remains the control point

The workflow can organize multimodal information, provide a second-reader perspective and help quantify local operational value. It does not replace a qualified clinician, institutional protocol, BI-RADS assessment, regulatory obligations, diagnosis or medical advice.
